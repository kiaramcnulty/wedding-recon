 I&#8217;ve spent the last decade photographing and filming elopements 
 across Colorado — from Guanella Pass above Georgetown 
 to the red rock formations of Garden of the Gods , from 
 remote cabins above Aspen to overlooks above Keystone and the mountain passes of Telluride .
 Elopements are the work I find most meaningful. There&#8217;s no 
 performance, no crowd, no schedule running twenty minutes behind. 
 Just two people, a landscape that matters to them, and the kind of 
 intimacy that a hundred-person wedding can&#8217;t replicate. My job is 
 to document that without getting in the way of it.
 I work with couples planning everything from a true two-person 
 ceremony in the backcountry to a micro-wedding with a small group 
 of people who matter most. Whatever the scale, the approach 
 is the same — intentional, unhurried, and deeply personal. 
 — Mark Blake 
 Where I Work Colorado Elopement Locations
01 Guanella Pass
 Georgetown, CO · Alpine Tundra One of Colorado&#8217;s most dramatic and accessible high alpine locations. 
 Sitting above 11,000 feet, Guanella Pass offers sweeping tundra 
 landscapes, wildflower meadows in summer, and golden aspen corridors 
 in fall — all within 90 minutes of Denver. One of my favorite 
 elopement locations in the state.
 ~90 min from Denver · Permit may be required 
 02 Sapphire Point Overlook
 Keystone, CO · Summit County A designated elopement-friendly location in Summit County with 
 panoramic views of the Ten Mile Range and Dillon Reservoir. 
 Sapphire Point is one of the few public locations in Colorado 
 that actively welcomes elopement ceremonies — accessible, 
 stunning, and genuinely special.
 Free · No permit required for elopements 
 03 Garden of the Gods
 Colorado Springs, CO · Red Rock The iconic red sandstone formations of Garden of the Gods are 
 unlike anything else in Colorado. Free, permit-free for personal 
 ceremonies, and visually unlike anywhere else in the state. 
 I&#8217;ve documented engagement sessions and ceremonies here and know the locations and timing that make each one exceptional.
 Free · No permit required · Personal use 
 04 Aspen & Snowmass
 Roaring Fork Valley · Remote Cabins The Roaring Fork Valley is one of my most active elopement 
 markets — from intimate cabin ceremonies above Aspen like the Smith Cabin to backcountry settings accessible only on foot or horseback. 
 The combination of altitude, isolation, and landscape makes 
 Aspen elopements some of the most visually compelling work I do.
 Film available · Full Aspen guide → 
 05 Telluride
 San Juan Mountains · Remote & Cinematic Telluride is in a category of its own for elopements — remote, 
 visually extraordinary, and completely removed from the 
 mainstream. I&#8217;ve filmed destination elopements in the Puente 
 Cabin area above town and throughout the surrounding San Juan 
 backcountry. If you want something that feels genuinely unlike 
 anything else, Telluride delivers.
 Film available · Full Telluride guide → 
 06 Rocky Mountain National Park
 Estes Park, CO · Alpine Wilderness One of the most searched elopement destinations in the entire 
 country — and for good reason. RMNP offers an extraordinary 
 range of settings from alpine meadows and glacier lakes to 
 tundra at 12,000 feet. Permits are required and fill up fast, 
 so early planning is essential for anyone considering an 
 elopement here.
 NPS Permit required · Book early 
 07 Vail & Beaver Creek
 Eagle County · Mountain Village Beyond the grand venue weddings, Vail and Beaver Creek offer 
 remarkable intimate ceremony settings — gondola-accessible 
 overlooks, mountain meadows, and private trailside locations 
 that most couples never discover. I know this valley well from 
 years of wedding work and can help you find a setting that 
 feels completely your own.
 Full Vail guide → 
 08 Flagstaff Mountain
 Boulder, CO · Front Range Just above Boulder, Flagstaff Mountain offers accessible 
 mountain terrain with sweeping views of the Front Range and 
 the city below. A strong option for couples based in Denver 
 or Boulder who want a mountain ceremony without a long drive 
 into the high country.
 ~45 min from Denver 
 09 Buena Vista & Salida
 Arkansas River Valley · Collegiate Peaks The Arkansas River Valley is an underrated elopement destination 
 with some of Colorado&#8217;s most dramatic Fourteener backdrops. 
 Buena Vista in particular — home to the Surf Hotel — 
 offers a compelling mix of mountain access, river scenery, 
 and a laid-back energy that attracts couples who want something 
 genuinely off the beaten path.
 ~2.5 hrs from Denver

Questions Elopement
 FAQ 
Do I need a permit to elope in Colorado? It depends on the location. Colorado state parks and national parks 
 like Rocky Mountain National Park require permits for ceremonies, 
 which should be secured well in advance as they fill up quickly. 
 Some locations like Sapphire Point Overlook are specifically 
 designated for elopements and require no permit. Garden of the Gods 
 requires no permit for personal photography or ceremonies. I help 
 every couple navigate permit requirements for their chosen location 
 as part of the planning process.
What is the best time of year to elope in Colorado? Late June through early October is the primary elopement season — 
 wildflowers peak in July, golden aspen runs mid-September through 
 early October, and the weather is generally the most reliable. 
 Winter elopements in snow are extraordinary but require more 
 logistical planning around access and conditions. Spring can be 
 unpredictable at altitude. September is personally my favorite 
 month in Colorado for elopement work — the light is incredible 
 and the crowds thin out after Labor Day.
Do you film elopements as well as photograph them? Yes — I offer both photography and film for elopements, and many 
 couples choose to combine both. Elopement films are particularly 
 meaningful for intimate ceremonies where the vows, the quiet 
 moments, and the emotion of the day are things you&#8217;ll want to 
 hear and feel again, not just see. Browse elopement films including Sabrina x Brenna&#8217;s Telluride cabin elopement and Ticha x Mike&#8217;s Sapphire Point ceremony on the motion page.
How far in advance should I book for a Colorado elopement? For peak season dates — July through early October — I&#8217;d recommend 
 reaching out at least six to nine months in advance, particularly 
 if you have a specific location in mind. Popular locations like 
 Rocky Mountain National Park issue a limited number of permits 
 that go quickly. Off-season elopements have more flexibility, 
 but I&#8217;d still encourage early contact to discuss location options 
 and logistics.
Can you help us choose an elopement location? Absolutely — and this is one of the parts of elopement planning 
 I genuinely enjoy. After a decade of working across Colorado&#8217;s 
 landscapes, I have strong opinions about which locations work 
 best at which times of year, which are overrun and which feel 
 genuinely remote, and which will photograph and film in a way 
 that holds up over time. I&#8217;m happy to make specific 
 recommendations based on your vision, your timeline, and your 
 physical comfort level with the terrain.
Do you travel for destination elopements outside Colorado? Yes. While Colorado is the primary focus of my elopement work, 
 I regularly travel for destination elopements across the American 
 West and abroad. Past elopement and intimate wedding work includes 
 locations in Utah, California, and internationally. If you&#8217;re 
 planning something outside Colorado, reach out through the booking page and let&#8217;s talk.
